SUBHASH PALEKAR ZERO BUDGET NATURAL FARMING (ZBNF)
Principles, Techniques, and Practices

== OVERVIEW ==

Subhash Palekar's Zero Budget Natural Farming (ZBNF) is a sustainable agricultural approach that eliminates synthetic chemicals while using locally-available resources. The method enables farmers to reduce input costs dramatically while maintaining soil health and productivity. Over 30 million farmers in India have adopted ZBNF.

== FOUR FOUNDATIONAL PILLARS ==

1. JEEVAMRUTHA (Living Elixir)
A microbial soil inoculant applied biweekly to enhance microbial populations and nutrient cycling.

Recipe (per 200-liter batch):
- 10 kg fresh cow dung (from indigenous/desi cow)
- 10 liters cow urine (from indigenous/desi cow)
- 2 kg jaggery (gud) — any grade works
- 2 kg pulse flour (gram flour/besan, or any legume flour)
- A handful of uncontaminated forest/bund soil (source of native microbes)
- 200 liters water

Preparation: Mix all ingredients in a large drum. Stir well. Ferment for 48 hours (2 days) in shade, stirring twice daily. A thin froth layer indicates fermentation is active.

Application:
- Dilute 200 liters of Jeevamrutha in 200 liters of water for 1 acre
- Apply every 15 days through drip irrigation or watering can
- Can also be used as foliar spray (dilute 1:10 with water)
- Results visible within 3-6 months: soil becomes darker, crumbly, earthy smell returns
- Single desi cow can serve 30 acres of farmland

2. BEEJAMRUTHA (Seed Treatment)
Natural seed treatment protecting seeds against soil-borne diseases and stimulating germination.

Recipe:
- 5 kg fresh cow dung
- 5 liters cow urine
- 50 grams lime (chuna)
- A handful of soil from farm bund
- 20 liters water

Method:
- Mix ingredients. Soak seeds for 15-30 minutes.
- Remove seeds, dry in shade (not direct sun) for 30 minutes.
- Sow immediately or within a few hours.

Benefits:
- Accelerates germination by 2-3 days
- Strengthens seedling vigor and root development
- Reduces soil-borne pest and fungal incidence
- Provides a protective microbial coating on seeds

3. ACCHADANA (Mulching)
Mulching practice that conserves moisture, reduces weeds, and improves soil structure.

Materials: Crop residues, green leaves, straw, dried leaves, or cover crops

Method: Layer 2-4 inches of mulch over soil surface around plants

Benefits:
- Prevents water evaporation (saves up to 50% irrigation water)
- Reduces weed emergence naturally
- Maintains cooler soil temperature in summer
- Minimizes soil erosion from rain
- Improves soil structure as mulch decomposes
- Encourages beneficial insects, fungi, and earthworms
- Fallen leaves from trees are excellent mulch — free resource

Types:
a) Soil mulch: Shallow surface scratching to break capillary and reduce evaporation
b) Straw mulch: Dry crop residues spread over soil
c) Live mulch: Low-growing cover crops (cowpea, groundnut) between main crop rows

4. WHAPASA (Soil Moisture-Air Balance)
The concept that soil health depends on optimal balance between water molecules and air molecules in soil pores.

Key Principles:
- Soil should be neither waterlogged nor bone dry
- Roots need both moisture AND oxygen to absorb nutrients
- Excess water = anaerobic conditions, root rot, no nutrient uptake
- ZBNF aims for "field capacity" — soil moist but not wet

Irrigation strategy for Whapasa:
- Water at midday in alternate furrows only
- This creates a moisture gradient — one side wet, one side drier
- Roots grow toward moisture, pulling air from drier side
- Much less water needed than flood irrigation
- Drip irrigation is ideal for achieving Whapasa

Enhancement: Add biological inoculants (Azotobacter, Rhizobium, Pseudomonas) to maintain soil biological equilibrium

== ZERO-BUDGET PHILOSOPHY ==

The "zero budget" means the cost of farming becomes nearly zero because:
- All inputs come from the farm itself (cow dung, urine, crop residues)
- One desi cow + 30 acres = completely self-sufficient
- No purchased fertilizers, pesticides, or seeds (use saved seeds)
- A single gram of desi cow dung contains up to 500 billion beneficial microorganisms

Comparison with Conventional Farming:
- ZBNF input cost: Rs. 4,000-10,000 per hectare
- Conventional input cost: Rs. 30,000-50,000 per hectare
- Yield: Comparable or better after 3-year transition
- Long-term: ZBNF soil improves every year; chemical soil degrades

== INDIGENOUS COW IMPORTANCE ==

Palekar specifically recommends DESI (indigenous) cows over HF/Jersey breeds:
- Desi cow dung has far more diverse and active microorganisms
- Desi cow urine contains 36+ proven bioactive compounds
- Desi cows are A2 milk producers (more nutritious)
- Best breeds: Gir, Sahiwal, Kangayam, Ongole, Rathi, Hallikar
- Even a neighbor's desi cow is sufficient — you don't need to own one

== SPIRITUAL-SCIENTIFIC INTEGRATION ==

ZBNF views agriculture as both scientific discipline and spiritual practice:
- Farmers are "stewards, not masters" of the land
- Respect natural rhythms (seasons, moon cycles for planting)
- Farm as a living organism, not a production factory
- Continuous learning and observation
- Long-term ecological responsibility for next generations

== ADOPTION IN INDIA ==

- Andhra Pradesh: 600,000+ farmers adopted ZBNF under state government program
- Karnataka: Major training programs through state agriculture department
- Himachal Pradesh: Entire state announced ZBNF transition
- Maharashtra, Rajasthan, Madhya Pradesh: Significant farmer groups

Results reported:
- 40-60% reduction in input costs
- Comparable or better yields after 2-3 year transition
- Improved soil health indicators (earthworm count, soil organic matter)
- Reduced farmer debt
- Better crop resilience to drought and pest pressure

== TRANSITION GUIDANCE ==

Year 1: Apply ZBNF practices on 25% of land; continue conventional on rest
Year 2: Expand ZBNF to 50% of land; reduce chemical inputs
Year 3: Full ZBNF transition; yields stabilize at 90-100% of previous
After Year 3: Yields often exceed conventional as soil health builds

Common fears and answers:
Q: Will my yield drop?
A: Initially may reduce 10-20% for 1-2 years, then recovers and often exceeds original

Q: Is ZBNF only for small farmers?
A: No — works at any scale. Large farms use tractor-mounted Jeevamrutha sprayers

Q: Do I need to buy anything?
A: Only jaggery and pulse flour for Jeevamrutha — total cost under Rs. 200 per preparation
