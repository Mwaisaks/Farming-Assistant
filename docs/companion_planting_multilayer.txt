COMPANION PLANTING AND MULTILAYER FARMING FOR INDIA
Natural Farming Education Resource

== COMPANION PLANTING (SATHI FASAL / SAHBAGI KHETI) ==

Companion planting means growing different plants together so they help each other — like good neighbors sharing resources and protecting each other.

WHY IT WORKS:
- Strong-smelling plants confuse pests searching for their target crop
- Nitrogen-fixing plants (beans, peas) enrich soil for neighbors
- Different root depths mean plants don't compete underground
- Flowers attract beneficial insects that eat crop pests
- Dense planting blocks weeds naturally

== CLASSIC INDIAN COMPANION PLANTING COMBINATIONS ==

1. TEEN BAHANEN (Three Sisters) — Maize + Beans + Pumpkin
   Traditional farming, perfect for Kharif season (June-October)
   - Maize/Bhutta: tall stalks give beans a pole to climb
   - Beans/Lobiya: roots fix nitrogen from air into soil, feeding maize
   - Pumpkin/Kaddu: big leaves spread on ground, keeping weeds down and soil moist
   One family of plants feeding and protecting each other — just like three sisters!
   How to plant: First plant maize. After 2 weeks, plant beans at maize base. After 2 more weeks, plant pumpkin in gaps.

2. Tomato + Basil/Tulsi
   - Tulsi repels aphids, whiteflies, and spider mites from tomatoes
   - Plant 1 tulsi for every 4-5 tomato plants
   - Attracts pollinators (bees) that improve tomato fruit set
   - Tulsi tea (leaves in water) can also be sprayed on tomatoes as pest repellent

3. Marigold (Genda Phool) + ANY Vegetable
   - PLANT MARIGOLD EVERYWHERE — it is the best companion plant for India
   - Roots release chemicals that kill soil nematodes (root-knot disease)
   - Strong smell confuses and repels aphids, whiteflies, thrips
   - Attracts beneficial insects (hoverflies, parasitic wasps)
   - Easy to grow; blooms in 6-8 weeks; no extra care needed
   Rule: Plant 1 row of marigold border for every vegetable plot

4. Mustard + Wheat (Sarson + Gehun)
   - Plant 1 row mustard for every 25 rows wheat
   - Aphids prefer mustard over wheat — it is a TRAP CROP
   - Aphids concentrate on mustard; when mustard is infested, remove it with aphids
   - Wheat stays clean without any pesticide
   This is traditional wisdom from Punjab and Haryana farmers

5. Onion/Garlic + Vegetables
   - Strong smell of onion and garlic repels many insects
   - Plant garlic between tomato rows, around brinjal, near roses
   - Garlic also has antifungal properties — reduces soil diseases

6. Pigeon Pea + Sorghum (Arhar + Jowar)
   - Traditional intercropping of Deccan plateau (Maharashtra, Karnataka, AP)
   - Arhar/Tur: tall legume, fixes nitrogen for jowar
   - Jowar: uses nitrogen provided by arhar
   - Both mature at different times; staggered harvest
   - Standard ratio: 2 jowar rows + 1 arhar row

7. Sugarcane + Short-Duration Vegetables
   - Plant garlic, onion, or leafy vegetables between sugarcane rows
   - Sugarcane takes 12-18 months; vegetables give income in 2-3 months
   - Maximizes land use; garlic repels sugarcane pests

8. Coriander/Dill as Insectary Plants
   - Let some coriander and dill flower (go to seed)
   - Tiny flowers attract parasitic wasps and hoverflies
   - These insects lay eggs in aphids and caterpillars — nature's pest control
   - Plant at field borders or scatter throughout

9. Groundnut + Sorghum/Millet
   - Groundnut fixes nitrogen; sorghum uses it
   - Sorghum provides partial shade; groundnut benefits from less intense sun
   - Common in dryland farming areas of Maharashtra, Rajasthan, MP

PLANTS TO KEEP SEPARATE:
- Onion/Garlic AWAY from beans and peas (onion chemicals inhibit legumes)
- Fennel/Saunf AWAY from most vegetables (releases growth-inhibiting chemicals)
- Sunflower AWAY from potato (competes and inhibits)

== MULTILAYER FARMING (BAHUSTAR KHETI) ==

Multilayer farming copies how a natural forest works. In a forest, tall trees, medium trees, shrubs, and ground cover all grow together — using different levels of sunlight, air, water, and soil. We design our farm the same way.

A SIMPLE ANALOGY:
Imagine a multi-story house. Tall crops are the top floor. Medium crops are middle floor. Ground crops are ground floor. Root crops are the basement. Everyone lives together, sharing the same land, wasting nothing.

THE FIVE LAYERS:

Layer 1 — TALL CANOPY (10-30 feet high):
Coconut, Banana (in tropical areas), Mango, Teak, Moringa/Drumstick, Papaya, Jackfruit
- Capture the highest sunlight
- Provide shade for lower layers
- Deep roots bring minerals from deep soil

Layer 2 — MEDIUM TREES (6-15 feet high):
Guava, Lemon/Citrus, Pomegranate, Amla, Curry leaf, Betel nut
- Grow in partial shade of tall canopy
- Harvest at different time than tall trees

Layer 3 — SHRUBS AND VINES (3-8 feet high):
Pineapple, Black pepper (climbing), Turmeric, Ginger, Cardamom, Chilli
- Many spice crops naturally prefer partial shade
- Add diversity and high-value crops in same space

Layer 4 — GROUND LAYER (0-3 feet high):
Pumpkin, Watermelon, Sweet potato, Vegetables, Leafy greens, Cowpea
- Spreads on ground between trees
- Utilizes ground-level light and space
- Acts as living mulch — keeps soil moist and weed-free

Layer 5 — UNDERGROUND / ROOT LAYER:
Yam (Suran), Colocasia (Arbi), Groundnut, Radish, Carrot, Garlic, Onion
- Grow completely underground
- No competition with above-ground crops for light
- Harvested separately without disturbing other layers

BENEFITS OF MULTILAYER FARMING:
✓ Maximum use of sunlight at every level
✓ Income from multiple crops throughout year (no single-harvest dependency)
✓ Permanent soil cover = less evaporation, less erosion
✓ Deep roots bring nutrients from subsoil layers
✓ Natural pest management (biodiversity confuses pests)
✓ Fallen leaves create automatic mulch — free soil improvement
✓ Microclimate creation — cooler, more humid within the system

== PRACTICAL MULTILAYER SYSTEMS FOR INDIA ==

System 1: COCONUT-BASED (Kerala, Coastal Karnataka, Goa)
- Coconut palms (25-30 feet)
- Banana + areca nut (10-15 feet, planted between coconuts)
- Black pepper vines (climbs coconut trunks)
- Pineapple (understory, prefers shade)
- Yam/colocasia (root layer)
Result: 5-6 income streams from same 1 acre; continuous year-round income

System 2: MANGO-BASED (North India, Vidarbha)
- Mango trees (main crop, 20-25 years return)
- Papaya + guava (medium layer, income in 1-2 years while waiting for mango)
- Turmeric/ginger (ground layer, prefers mango shade)
- Vegetables in sunny gaps
- Fodder grass on bunds for cattle
Result: Regular income within first year while building long-term mango orchard

System 3: MORINGA (DRUMSTICK) SYSTEM — Best for smallholders
- Plant moringa in rows 10 feet apart (fast growing, ready in 6-8 months)
- Grow vegetables (tomato, brinjal, leafy greens) between rows
- Moringa leaves are nutritious (both for family and as green manure)
- Drumsticks are high-value vegetable crop
- Dried moringa leaf powder can be sold as health supplement
- Income from vegetables + drumsticks + leaves/powder
Result: Maximum production from small land; moringa is drought tolerant once established

System 4: WADI MODEL (Tribal India — Maharashtra, Gujarat, Rajasthan)
- Traditional multilayer system of tribal and marginal farmers
- Fruit trees (main investment: 5-7 year horizon)
- Forest trees for timber/fuel (long-term)
- Annual crops in spaces between trees (current income)
- Used by BAIF Development Research Foundation and PRADAN in India
- Provides food, income, fuel, and biodiversity on 0.4-1 acre plots

== SOIL HEALTH IN MULTILAYER SYSTEMS ==

COVER CROPS (Dhakne Wali Fasal):
Plant these during fallow periods or between rows of main crop:
- Dhaincha (Sesbania): Grows fast; 1.5-2 tons dry matter in 45 days; excellent nitrogen fixer
- Sunn Hemp: Similar to dhaincha; 6-8% nitrogen in biomass
- Horse gram (Kulthi): Drought-tolerant; good for dryland areas
- Cowpea (Lobiya): Fixes nitrogen; edible pods also available
How to use: Grow for 45-60 days; cut and incorporate into soil (green manure) 2-3 weeks before planting main crop

EARTHWORM INDICATOR:
Count earthworms in 30x30 cm, 30 cm deep soil sample:
- Less than 5 earthworms: poor soil health — start ZBNF immediately
- 5-15 earthworms: improving — continue organic practices
- 15+ earthworms: healthy soil — you're on the right track
Earthworms disappear from chemically-treated soil; their return is the best sign that your soil is recovering

ROLE OF TREES ON FARM BUNDS:
- Plant local trees on field boundaries (bunds): pipal, neem, drumstick, curry leaf, karanj
- Roots hold bunds against erosion
- Fallen leaves are free mulch
- Shade reduces water evaporation near field edges
- Habitat for beneficial birds and insects
- Karanj (Pongamia) pods are valuable biopesticide material

== SEASONAL CALENDAR FOR COMPANION PLANTING (NORTH INDIA) ==

KHARIF (June-October): Maize+Beans+Pumpkin; Paddy+Arhar; Groundnut+Jowar
RABI (November-February): Wheat+Mustard+Chickpea; Garlic intercropped with vegetables
ZAID (March-May): Watermelon+Maize; Cucumber+Cowpea; Vegetables with marigold borders

KEY PRINCIPLES TO REMEMBER:
1. Plant diversity is strength — monoculture invites pest outbreaks
2. Always maintain soil cover — bare soil is unhealthy soil
3. Every fallow period is an opportunity for cover crops or green manure
4. Trees are not competitors — they are partners and long-term investments
5. Observe your farm like a student — each year you will learn something new
